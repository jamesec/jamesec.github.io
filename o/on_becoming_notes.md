## Book notes
### pp. 215-216
Standing on the shoulders of Rogers, Gendlin, Rice, and Perls, I thus developed a theoretical approach to working with emotion in psychotherapy and a theory of practice that changed the focus from their earlier forms of practice. Rather than seeing therapy as being based solely on empathy, deepening experience, expanding information processing, or promoting awareness, I came to see that it was the allowing of painful emotion and its productive processing and transformation that was the core process. When working with maladaptive emotion, acceptance plus change was what was called for.

Whereas Rogers offered an interpersonal process to facilitate the emergence of the actualizing tendency, which was based on the organismic valuing process, I came to hypothesize that emotion, which provides automatic appraisal of situation in relation to needs, was in fact the organismic valuing process. Whereas Gendlin, proposed a process of guiding clients to focus on a bodily felt sense that led to the creation of new meaning by symbolizing the bodily felt sense in words, I claimed that the felt sense was formed from the automatic synthesis of many internal emotion related processes in an internal working zone of complexity (Greenberg & Pascual-Leone, 2001). Whereas Perls proposed present-centered awareness that would lead to the rising awareness of the most urgent need, I, by contrast, suggested concentration on aroused emotion would lead to the emergence of the need.

In relation to how this approach evolved, I relied both on my explicit theoretical learnings, mainly from the humanistic and experiential theories, but I also was somewhat influenced by psychodynamic theory. The explicit ideas, however, were strongly shaped by following my own intuitive or implicit sense of what worked in practice coupled with research-based observation of how people changed in actual therapy sessions.

### p. 391
HOW TO BECOME MORE EMOTION FOCUSED: 100 TIPS → Ancillary Activities
- `96.` Personal work is the royal road to emotional competence.
- `97.` Supervision is the best way to acquire skills.
- `98.` Watch video recordings of experienced therapists.
- `99.` Watch your own recordings.
- `100.` Track the client–therapist moment-by-moment interactions on your own recordings using rating scales such as the depth of experience, expressed emotional arousal, and vocal quality scales.
