## 001 What is Emotion-Focused Therapy (EFT)?
EFT stands for Emotion-Focused Therapy, which is not the same as Emotion Freedom Technique, but essentially it’s a therapy that’s focuses on emotion and it sees emotion as… at the… the basic, um, datum of human experience. That’s the very first thing we feel in the world. 

And so it’s a way of working with emotion, but it has two important components, which is we work with a relationship, with an empathic relationship, but we also have different, uh, interventions for different kinds of problems that arise in the session. 

So I like to think about it as, um, islands of work within an ocean of empathy and these islands of work are, have been studied and that what we call marker guided, um, interventions. 

A person in a session reaches a particular state, like a self-criticism or unfinished business with a significant other. And then we have ways of intervening. So these markers are opportunities for different kinds of interventions. 

So we work with emotions. The relationship is really important and we have different interventions for different in-session problem states.

## 002 Six principles for working with emotions
It’s very important in working with emotion to know what you’re doing. So we’ve developed six principles for working with emotion, which are based on sort of evidence of how to work with emotion. And these are that we help people to be aware of emotion. Being aware of emotion turns what’s passive or happens to you, into something that you’re an agent of. You now know what you’re feeling and it’s yours. So the first principle is awareness. The second principle is expressing emotion. And expressing is not the same as awareness. Expression is where we use a chair dialogue and you express it to somebody. But for example, in marital therapy, saying to the therapist, I love my wife, is not the same as looking at your wife and saying, I love you. And that sort of captures the difference or the power of what expression is. And then there’s emotion regulation, which is both… how to when emotion is too overwhelming, how to help people move it back to a a level at which they can process it.  

And then there’s reflecting on emotion. This is where insight, understanding, understanding patterns, having creating new narratives occurs. This is where you reflect on your emotional experience. But then the two most important principles have to do with transformation of emotion. And the first one is changing emotion with emotion. And here the notion is that you can’t really change an amygdala based emotion with reason that it’s impenetrable to reason and the way to change emotion is with another powerful… more powerful or stronger emotion. And then the final principle is the corrective emotional experience, which also involves changing emotion with emotion. But now it’s emotion in the interpersonal relationship. And in therapy it’s often to do with the therapist. So if I expect that I’ll be shamed when I say something to the therapist, but I in fact experienced being valued and validated. That’s changing in emotion, but it’s coming from a real relationship. So these are six principles that are very important in working with emotion.

## 004 What is the difference between feelings and emotions?
There are three words that people use to describe emotional, talk about it, affect, emotion and feeling. And it’s very complicated because different people use them in different ways. 

But I think the best way of thinking it is sort of with an image is if you think of, uh, the whole emotion as a tree, you have the roots and the main stem, and that’s affect, it’s the sort of physiological basis, excitement, arousal, adrenaline.

And then the main branches of the tree, or categorical emotions, which are emotions, anger, sadness, fear, shame, joy, love.

## 005 What is the difference between primary and secondary emotions?
Primary and secondary emotions are a key concept in EFT. And a primary emotion is the very first emotion a person feels in a situation. It’s not a basic emotion like anger, sadness, that’s evolutionary basic. It’s just the very first emotion. So I could feel envious, which is a complex emotion as my very first feeling when I see you have something I want. Um, and it’s usually an adaptive emotion. Uh, and we live by these primary emotions, but then a secondary emotion is an emotion that comes second. 

And so the example we all probably know is that often when people feel hurt, they get angry and they express anger. So really the anger is masking the hurt. And in sexual stereotype terms, men who have difficulty with vulnerable feelings like fear and shame, often express anger. So we see anger, but really they’re feeling underneath shame or fear. Women in sexual stereotype terms often cry when they’re angry. They’ve been socialized not to express anger, so they cry. So we see these secondary emotions obscure or get in the way of getting to what’s really being experienced. 

So we want to get past secondary emotions to the most fundamental emotions that people feel. One of the main things is like, why are primary emotions important? Well, emotions are most fundamentally action tendencies. So fear organizes me to run away, in anger I puff up and thrust forward. So emotion is my basic orienting system to the world. In addition, anger… uh, emotions gives me information about my reactions. So I start to organize, to run away, and the emotion is telling me I am in danger. So we really need to help people get to their primary emotions ’cause they give action tendency. They give important information, important to my survival. And emotions also are signaling systems. They communicate. So it’s very important that people be clear and able to use their information, their emotion, uh, adaptively because if they don’t, they are a system that is feeling something but not aware of it or letting it inform them.

## 006 What is core pain?
Well, as well as primary and secondary emotion, which is one discrimination. We have a distinction that a primary emotion is either adaptive or maladaptive. And this maladaptive emotion is what is better called core pain. So people care… Emotions are fundamentally adaptive, my very first response usually helps me orient. But if I have a traumatic learning or I’ve suffered neglect, abandonment, abuse, my emotion system no longer becomes adaptive. 

So in post-traumatic stress disorder, a soldier might hear a door bang and may duck to hide from the bullet. The emotional brain is telling him that danger is present, but there is no danger present. So the emotion has become maladaptive and has become a core painful emotion that influences people in their lives. 

So the emotion was probably adaptive in the first instance, but it was never dealt with properly, and so it’s remained as a very painful feeling. So we believe that it’s important. The client knows where they hurt, they know much better than I do, where they hurt and what hurts. So we wanna follow their pain and that will lead us to their core painful issue.

## 009 Changing emotion with emotion
So changing emotion with emotion is the most novel concept, I think in EFT of how people change. Um, Spinoza, philosopher in the 1600s wrote, the only way to change an emotion is with a stronger and opposing emotion. And this is, uh, a principle that we adopt as a central principle. I don’t believe you can change amygdala-activated core painful emotions through reason. They’re impenetrable to reason.

So the way to change a core painful emotion is by having another emotion, which then helps transform. Now, it’s not replacing, you don’t replace the one emotion with the other emotion, but the new emotion transforms or synthesizes with the old emotion. And then we have a new emerging emotion, which we didn’t feel before. So we can think shame has changed by anger, and what you end up with is, say confidence. Fear might be changed by sadness, and what we end up with is calm or relief. There’s one emotion changes another emotion.

And if you think of it very basically, if the action tendency in fear or shame is to withdraw, to run away or to shrink into the ground, the action tendency in anger is to thrust forward or in sadness is to cry out for the lost object. You can’t run away and thrust forward at the same time. So if, when you are in deep shame or fear, I can help anger emerge, or grief and sadness emerge, the one is gonna blend with the other and they’re gonna transform. So it’s a little bit like, as a metaphor, if you have a set of, um, hormones or neurochemicals flowing in your blood when you’re ashamed, if you begin to get angry, you’ll have different neurochemicals, different wirings in your brain. So now in your bloodstream you have this mixture of neurochemicals and it forms a new state. Now that’s just an analogy, right? Not a, uh, explicit scientific description, but it gives you the feeling of how one emotion undoes and transforms another emotion.

## 010 How does Emotion-Focused Therapy (EFT) relate to mental health problems?
EFT sees mental health problems as being based on core painful emotions and that people have had emotions which were not responded to in their life, or not processed by them in their life in an adequate way. And that the ways they then dealt with their emotion are not very helpful. So they have negative ways of dealing with their emotion, which they experience as protecting themselves from their emotions. But in the end, people disclaim or disown their emotional experience. Now that doesn’t mean they don’t know what they feel, but they don’t own it or claim it as theirs, which means they don’t experience their emotions.

So I think underneath every mental health problem, major component of many mental health problems is emotion that hasn’t been processed. And that means, and I use my hand, it’s been kept at bay as a protection because people are afraid that if they feel their emotions, they’ll be overwhelmed by them, they’ll disintegrate. So actually they try to cope with, but their painful emotions by staying away from them. But that becomes the problem.

So anxiety is like a smoke detector that tells you that danger is potentially there, but when it gets turned to my emotions might explode. Um, then it becomes dysfunctional. It’s no longer working. So now we get very anxious and panicky, but really we’re afraid that our emotions will erupt and that we won’t be able to cope. So anxiety is actually a type of attempted way of coping that I don’t fall apart. Uh, but it’s not helpful and similar in other disorders. At the core of it is that I am disclaiming my emotions and not really processing them, but they keep coming up trying to get attention. But my negative ways of dealing with my emotion are mental health problems. I get depressed, I get anxious, I have eating disorders, I calm myself, all of these things I’ve done in order to try to, dysfunctionally try to cope with painful emotions that I’m afraid will lead to my disintegration.

And therapy is a process of helping people face those emotions and go to the need in them, which is actually an adaptive, healthy need, and that helps them, and then bringing in new emotions. So the cause of major mental health problems are emotions that people are afraid of and try to stay away from. But the cure is really being able to approach those emotions and symbolize them in awareness, deal with them, deal with the needs that they express.

## 011 Depression and relationship difficulties explained by Emotion-Focused Therapy (EFT)
So in EFT, if somebody comes in with depression or somebody comes in and says, I just don’t get on with other people, or I have difficulty with other people, we are going to try to take them to the core underlying emotion that generates those problems.

So in depression, there might be either a core feeling of shame, like, I’m a failure, I’m worthless, or a core feeling of abandonment or unlovedness. But the depression is a secondary symptom where…because the need has not been met, the need for love or the need for validation, they begin to feel hopeless that the need will ever be met. They give up and then they feel depressed, right? Because they sort of given up on trying to get the need met and feel that it’ll never happen. But really underneath their feeling core shame of feeling worthless or core lonely abandonment, like nobody will ever be there or love me.

And it’s those feelings that manifest at the surface of as depression. But the symptom is a secondary phenomena. The dis-ease is the underlying shame or fear. Similarly with people who have difficulty in relationships, they might at core feel afraid of opening themselves because then they will get rejected, which is kind of shame-based. Or they might feel nobody’s ever been there for me, so I’m unlovable. Uh, and what we see is difficulties in relationship where they’re wary and they don’t take any kind…they don’t open themselves at all in relationship, or they’re aggressive in relationships because they always feel wronged.

But the core problem is always the underlying emotion, and that’s what we have to get to. But there are many layers. People come in with concrete problems and difficulties, uh, but we don’t try to treat the symptom. I mean, we validate and understand the painfulness of the symptom, but we are trying to get to the more primary underlying emotion.

## 013 What is chair work in Emotion-Focused Therapy (EFT)? Les Greenberg
In EFT, we use chair work. And chair work is where it’s a psychodramatic use, um, of having a dialogue with an empty chair. And you imagine other parts of yourself or other people in chairs. And the thing that chair work adds is a type of dramatization, but really it uses what I would call expression, where you express something. And when human beings express something in words, it evokes experience. And so it’s a use of different chairs, particularly self-critical two chair work, where you have a critical voice, your own critical voice criticizing you, or empty chair work, which is unfinished business with a significant other where you imagine somebody else in an empty chair. And that usually immediately evokes feelings. And then you express these feelings to the other. So these chair works help evoke more feeling and help you to work through these feelings.
